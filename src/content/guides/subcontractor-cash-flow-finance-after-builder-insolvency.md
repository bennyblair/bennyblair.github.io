Subcontractor cash flow finance after builder insolvency is commercial funding considered when a subcontractor is owed money, retentions are delayed, or work-in-progress payments are disrupted because a head contractor or builder has entered external administration. Finance may help bridge timing pressure, but it does not fix a bad debt by itself and should be assessed alongside legal, insolvency, and accounting advice.

For subcontractors, the main issue is timing and evidence. A lender wants to understand what is owed, who owes it, whether the claim is disputed, what security exists, what other jobs are continuing, and how the facility will be repaid. If the debt is uncertain or the business has no replacement cash flow, borrowing can add risk instead of solving the problem.

This guide explains the finance options subcontractors may consider after builder insolvency, what documents matter, when invoice or debtor finance may fit, when property-backed finance may be considered, and when legal advice should come before a loan application.

## Who This Is For

This guide is for construction subcontractors, trades, civil contractors, fitout businesses, suppliers, and project-service businesses affected by builder insolvency or delayed project payments.

It is not legal advice, insolvency advice, or financial advice. If a builder has entered administration, liquidation, or receivership, get advice from a qualified professional before relying on any expected recovery.

## Citation-Ready Answer: What Finance Can Help After Builder Insolvency?

After builder insolvency, subcontractors may consider working capital finance, invoice finance, debtor finance, asset-backed finance, or property-secured commercial finance to manage temporary cash-flow pressure. Lenders assess the strength of unpaid claims, whether debts are disputed, the subcontractor's remaining revenue, available security, current liabilities, and the repayment plan. Finance is more viable when the business has ongoing work and credible recovery or refinance options; it is higher risk when the unpaid amount is uncertain or the business has no replacement cash flow. ## Why Builder Insolvency Hits Subcontractors Hard

Builder insolvency can freeze payments at exactly the wrong time. Subcontractors may have already paid labour, materials, equipment hire, insurance, and supplier invoices before receiving final progress payments.

The cash-flow gap can widen quickly when retention money is withheld, variations are disputed, or a project administrator pauses payments while claims are reviewed. Even profitable subcontractors can face pressure if several projects are connected to the same failed builder.

The first step is to separate recoverable timing pressure from unrecoverable loss. Finance can help with timing. It cannot turn a weak or disputed claim into certain cash.

## What to Do Before Applying for Finance

Before approaching lenders, gather the facts. Confirm the builder's status, the administrator or liquidator contact, the claim amount, the status of any progress claims, retention balances, variation claims, and whether any amount is disputed.

You should also check your own position. Are supplier accounts current? Are wages and super obligations up to date? Are tax debts manageable? Are other projects producing cash? Is the business able to trade safely while waiting for recovery?

If there is uncertainty about your rights, payment priority, or insolvency process, legal or insolvency advice should come before a finance decision.

## Finance Options Subcontractors May Compare

The right option depends on the quality of the debt, the business's other cash flow, and available security.

[Working capital finance](/resources/guides/working-capital-loans-for-smes) may suit a subcontractor with strong ongoing jobs and a short-term liquidity gap. The lender looks at business trading, bank conduct, tax position, and the ability to repay from future revenue.

[Invoice finance](/resources/guides/invoice-finance-australia-complete-guide) may suit where invoices are owed by creditworthy debtors and are not disputed. It may be harder where the debtor is insolvent, the claim is uncertain, or the invoice depends on certification that has not happened.

Property-backed private finance may be considered where the subcontractor has usable property equity and a clear exit. That can include refinancing, sale proceeds, recovery of claims, or replacement project revenue, but the risk to property must be taken seriously.

## Progress Claims, Retentions, and Variations

Progress claims can be useful evidence, but lenders want to know whether they are certified, disputed, paid in part, or subject to set-off. A submitted claim is not the same as an accepted receivable.

Retention money also needs careful treatment. Retentions may be contractually owed, but recovery timing can be uncertain after insolvency. A lender may treat retention balances conservatively unless there is strong evidence of timing and entitlement.

Variations are often the hardest. If a variation has not been approved, the lender may discount it heavily or ignore it for borrowing-capacity purposes.

## When Invoice Finance May Not Work

Invoice finance is not always available after builder insolvency. If the debtor is insolvent, the invoice is disputed, or the administrator has not confirmed the claim, the invoice may not be financeable.

That does not mean the subcontractor has no options. It means the lender may need to assess other sources of repayment, such as ongoing projects, plant and equipment, property equity, or a broader debt restructure.

For subcontractors with several unpaid invoices across solvent customers, invoice finance may still help with the stronger part of the debtor ledger. The insolvent-builder debt can be treated separately.

## When Property-Backed Finance May Be Considered

Property-backed finance may be considered where the subcontractor needs urgent liquidity and has usable equity in commercial or investment property. The lender will usually focus on security, title position, existing mortgages, purpose, and exit.

This can be relevant when a subcontractor has strong future work but needs to pay suppliers, staff, tax, or equipment hire while claims are resolved. It can also be relevant where delayed payments would otherwise cause a cascade of defaults.

However, property-backed finance adds serious risk. If the expected recovery does not arrive, the borrower still has to repay the facility. For urgent, short-term structures, comparing [private lending](/resources/guides/what-is-private-lending-australia), [caveat loans](/resources/guides/caveat-loans-australia-complete-guide), and [commercial property refinance](/resources/guides/commercial-property-refinancing-solutions) is important.

## When Not To Borrow

Borrowing is usually unsuitable if the business has no credible repayment source, the debt is heavily disputed, the builder insolvency has removed most future work, or the subcontractor may already be insolvent.

It may also be unsuitable if the facility would mainly pay old creditors without stabilising future trading. In that situation, the business may need restructuring, legal advice, negotiation with suppliers, or formal insolvency advice before taking on more debt.

A loan should buy time for a viable business. It should not disguise a business that can no longer trade safely.

## Documents That Strengthen the File

A strong finance file tells the story clearly. Useful documents include the subcontract, purchase orders, progress claims, invoices, payment schedules, retention ledger, variation register, administrator notices, debtor ledger, creditor list, bank statements, BAS, ATO account position, equipment list, current job pipeline, and a written cash-flow forecast.

If the business has replacement work, include signed contracts, awarded tenders, purchase orders, or emails confirming start dates. Lenders are more comfortable when the exit does not depend entirely on an insolvent-builder recovery.

If property is offered as security, include title searches, mortgage statements, rates notices, leases if applicable, and any valuation evidence.

## Practical Scenario: Strong Subcontractor, One Failed Builder

A mechanical services subcontractor is owed money by a builder that has entered administration. The subcontractor still has several active jobs with solvent counterparties, a clean debtor ledger outside the failed builder, and a clear plan to reduce supplier pressure.

In this scenario, working capital finance or invoice finance against stronger receivables may help. The builder insolvency is a problem, but it is not the only source of cash flow.

The lender would still treat the insolvent-builder claim carefully. The strongest part of the application is the ongoing work and diversified revenue.

## Practical Scenario: All Revenue Tied to the Failed Builder

A subcontractor has most revenue tied to one failed builder, unpaid variations are disputed, and there is no confirmed replacement work. Suppliers are pressing, tax is overdue, and payroll is due within days.

This is a high-risk finance request. Borrowing may only delay a deeper problem if there is no realistic repayment pathway. Legal, accounting, and insolvency advice should be prioritised before any secured loan is considered.

## FAQs

### Can subcontractors get finance after a builder becomes insolvent?

Yes, some subcontractors can access commercial finance after builder insolvency, but approval depends on evidence, ongoing cash flow, available security, and the repayment plan. A disputed or uncertain claim against an insolvent builder may not be enough by itself.

### Can unpaid progress claims be financed?

Unpaid progress claims may support finance if they are certified, enforceable, owed by a creditworthy debtor, and not materially disputed. Claims linked to an insolvent builder are usually assessed cautiously and may need other repayment sources or security.

### Can retention money be used as security for finance?

Retention money may be considered as part of the cash-flow picture, but lenders often treat it conservatively because timing and recovery can be uncertain after insolvency. Legal advice may be needed to confirm entitlement and priority.

### Is invoice finance suitable after builder insolvency?

Invoice finance may be suitable for invoices owed by solvent, reliable customers. It may not work for invoices owed by an insolvent builder, disputed claims, or unapproved variations. The broader debtor ledger matters.

### When should a subcontractor avoid borrowing?

A subcontractor should be cautious about borrowing if there is no credible repayment source, no replacement work, a heavily disputed claim, or a risk the business is trading insolvent. Professional legal, accounting, or insolvency advice should come first in those cases.

### What documents do lenders ask for?

Lenders commonly ask for contracts, invoices, progress claims, payment schedules, retention records, debtor and creditor ledgers, bank statements, BAS, ATO position, current job pipeline, and evidence of security if property or assets are involved.

### Can property-secured finance help a subcontractor with urgent cash flow?

Property-secured finance may help where there is usable equity, a clear commercial purpose, and a realistic exit. It also creates property risk, so the borrower should compare alternatives and obtain professional advice before using property as security.
