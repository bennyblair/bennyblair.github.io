# Second Mortgage vs Line of Credit: Which to Choose?

A second mortgage and a line of credit can both help a business borrower access capital, but they solve different problems.

A second mortgage is usually a lump-sum facility secured behind an existing first mortgage. A line of credit is a revolving facility that lets you draw, repay, and redraw within an approved limit. On paper, both can unlock liquidity. In practice, the better fit depends on whether you need one defined amount for a clear commercial purpose or flexible access to funds over time.

For property investors, developers, and business owners, the real choice is about structure rather than labels. If you are funding a time-sensitive settlement, a partner buyout, a tax debt workout, or a defined business expansion cost, a [second mortgage](/resources/guides/first-and-second-mortgages-for-business) may be the cleaner tool. If you need ongoing working capital flexibility, staged project spend, or a buffer for uneven cash flow, a business [line of credit](/resources/guides/line-of-credit-equity) may be more practical.

## What is a second mortgage?

A second mortgage is a loan secured against property that already has a first mortgage registered on title. The second lender sits behind the first lender in repayment priority.

That ranking matters. If something goes wrong, the first mortgage is repaid before the second mortgage lender. Because of that, second mortgages are usually assessed carefully around available equity, exit strategy, and overall leverage.

In business lending, a second mortgage often appears when a borrower wants to raise a defined amount without disturbing an existing first mortgage structure. That can be useful where the current first mortgage terms are still attractive, but extra capital is needed for a commercial purpose.

## What is a line of credit?

A line of credit is a revolving funding limit rather than a single fully drawn term loan. Once approved, the borrower can usually draw funds up to the limit, repay some or all of the balance, and draw again if the facility terms allow it.

That flexibility is the main attraction. A line of credit can work well where funding needs move around rather than landing once. For example, a business might use it for uneven working capital, deposits, progress costs, or temporary liquidity pressure.

Some lines of credit are secured against property or business assets. Others are assessed more like standard commercial cash-flow facilities. The exact structure depends on lender type, security, and purpose.

## The core difference: lump sum versus revolving access

This is where most borrowers should start.

A second mortgage is usually best when you already know roughly how much capital is needed and what it is for. The lender can assess the security, amount, purpose, and exit in a fairly defined way.

A line of credit is usually best when you need funding flexibility rather than one fixed advance. If the business expects repeated drawings, variable cash conversion cycles, or uncertain timing, revolving access may be more useful than a single second mortgage advance.

## When a second mortgage may be the better fit

### You need a specific amount for a defined purpose

A second mortgage can suit a partner payout, tax debt resolution, business acquisition deposit, urgent settlement gap, or time-sensitive commercial obligation.

In these cases, flexibility matters less than speed, certainty, and enough capital to solve the actual problem.

### You want to preserve the existing first mortgage

Some borrowers do not want to refinance the whole debt stack just to raise extra capital. If the first mortgage remains competitive or hard to replace, a second mortgage may let you keep it in place.

### The exit is clear

Second mortgage lenders usually want a credible repayment path. That could be refinance, sale, retained business cash flow, or another defined capital event.

The clearer the exit, the easier it is to explain why a second mortgage is being used instead of a more open-ended facility.

## When a line of credit may be the better fit

### Cash flow moves around during the year

Some businesses do not have one large capital event. They have repeated short-term funding needs. That is where a line of credit can be more logical than taking a second mortgage and leaving part of it unused.

### Spending will happen in stages

A business with lumpy supplier payments, fit-out costs, or project staging may prefer to draw funds only when needed. A revolving facility can reduce the mismatch between borrowing and actual use.

### You want a liquidity buffer, not just one transaction

Some borrowers want a funding reserve in case opportunities or pressure points appear. A line of credit can be more appropriate if the real goal is optionality.

## How lenders think about each option

### Second mortgage assessment

A second mortgage lender will usually focus on:

- existing first mortgage balance
- available equity in the property
- total leverage across both facilities
- the commercial purpose of the loan
- the strength and timing of the exit

That is why a second mortgage often behaves like event-driven funding. The story needs to make sense from day one.

### Line of credit assessment

A line of credit lender will usually focus more heavily on:

- the business's trading profile and cash-flow pattern
- whether the facility will be used responsibly and within purpose
- security support, if property-backed
- ongoing repayment behaviour, not just final exit
- how the approved limit fits the scale of the business

This is one reason some borrowers find a line of credit conceptually attractive but harder to size than expected. Lenders are often cautious about giving flexible capital where usage discipline is unclear.

## Second mortgage vs line of credit for common business scenarios

### Scenario 1: Partner buyout

A company director needs $480,000 to buy out a departing shareholder. The business owns commercial property with enough equity, and the intention is to refinance the full structure within six months after the business stabilises.

A second mortgage is often the cleaner fit because the purpose, amount, and exit are all specific.

### Scenario 2: Seasonal working capital swings

A wholesaler has uneven inventory cycles and needs extra liquidity several times a year. The amount required changes depending on supplier timing and stock depth.

A line of credit is often more practical because the need is recurring rather than one-off.

### Scenario 3: Short settlement pressure

An investor needs funds quickly to complete a commercial settlement while another refinance is still being finalised. The amount required is known and the repayment event is expected once the refinance settles.

That is usually closer to second mortgage territory than line-of-credit territory because it is a defined bridge problem.

### Scenario 4: Staged fit-out and business expansion

A business is expanding into a larger premises and expects costs to arrive in waves across fit-out, equipment, and early operating capital.

A line of credit can be stronger if the spending path is uncertain and there is value in drawing only what is needed at each stage.

## When to use a second mortgage

### Use it when speed and certainty matter more than flexibility

If the problem is immediate and the amount is known, a second mortgage can be a focused solution.

### Use it when the funding is tied to one commercial event

Borrowers often use second mortgages for business-purpose events with a natural finish line.

### Use it when a clear exit already exists

Second mortgages work better when there is a visible way out rather than an indefinite reliance on the facility.

## When not to use a second mortgage

### Do not use it just because there is equity

Accessible property equity does not automatically mean a second mortgage is the right tool. If the need is ongoing and unpredictable, a revolving facility may fit better.

### Do not use it without a realistic repayment path

A second mortgage without a clear exit can turn from helpful to expensive very quickly.

## When to use a line of credit

### Use it when the business needs flexibility

If drawings and repayments are likely to move up and down, a line of credit usually has the better shape.

### Use it when you want funds available but not fully drawn upfront

That can be sensible where contingency, timing, or staged use matters.

## When not to use a line of credit

### Do not use it for a one-off event just because it sounds flexible

Flexibility is only helpful if you genuinely need it. For a defined one-time requirement, a second mortgage or other structured facility may be cleaner.

### Do not treat it like permanent spare cash

A line of credit still needs discipline, purpose, and a lender-approved use case. It is not a magic hole in the wall for business stress.

## Which is usually easier to get?

Neither is automatically easier. They are just assessed differently.

A second mortgage may be more workable when the property equity is strong and the exit is well defined. A line of credit may be more workable when the business has stable trading and needs recurring access to capital rather than a single event-driven advance.

Borrowers sometimes assume a line of credit is simpler because it sounds mainstream. That is not always true. A lender may be more comfortable with a tightly scoped second mortgage than an open-ended revolving request with vague usage.

## Questions to ask before choosing

### What exactly am I funding?

If you cannot explain the funding need cleanly, you probably should not choose a product yet.

### Is this a one-time event or an ongoing pattern?

That answer usually points strongly toward either second mortgage or line of credit.

### What is the realistic repayment path?

Even a flexible facility needs a sensible repayment story.

### Am I choosing flexibility because I need it, or because I am avoiding a sharper decision?

That question tends to cut through a lot of noise.

## Frequently asked questions

### What is the main difference between a second mortgage and a line of credit?

A second mortgage is usually a lump-sum property-backed facility used for a defined commercial purpose. A line of credit is a revolving limit that lets a borrower draw, repay, and redraw over time.

### Is a second mortgage better than a line of credit for business use?

Not automatically. A second mortgage often suits one-off commercial events with a clear amount and exit. A line of credit often suits recurring cash-flow needs, staged spend, or contingency funding.

### Can a line of credit be secured against property?

Potentially, yes. Some business lines of credit are secured against property or other assets, depending on lender policy and the borrower's profile.

### Why would a business choose a second mortgage instead of refinancing everything?

Sometimes the existing first mortgage is worth preserving, or the borrower only needs an additional defined amount rather than a full refinance. A second mortgage can solve that gap without rebuilding the entire debt stack.

### Is a line of credit cheaper than a second mortgage?

That depends on lender type, security, risk, and structure. The better question is whether the facility shape actually matches the job. A cheaper-looking product can still be the wrong choice if it does not suit the funding need.

### Can either option be used for personal or residential borrowing?

This guide is focused on commercial and business-purpose lending only. Emet Capital works with eligible business borrowers, not retail or owner-occupier residential lending.

## Bottom line

A second mortgage is usually the better tool when you need a defined amount for a defined commercial purpose and can point to a credible exit.

A line of credit is usually the better tool when the business needs flexibility, repeated access to funds, or a capital buffer that moves with trading conditions.

If the funding purpose is sharp, event-driven, and property-backed, second mortgage logic often wins. If the funding need is recurring and variable, a line of credit may be the more honest fit.
