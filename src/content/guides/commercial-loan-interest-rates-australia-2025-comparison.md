Commercial loan interest rates in Australia are the pricing terms attached to business-purpose lending. There is no single market rate that applies to every borrower. A lender prices the deal in front of it based on security, cash flow, loan purpose, document quality, leverage, industry risk, and how clearly the exit or repayment path makes sense.

That is why two businesses can ask for similar funding and still receive materially different offers. A straightforward owner-occupied property loan, an equipment-backed facility, and a short-term private refinance are all commercial loans, but they do not sit in the same risk bucket. The useful question is not just “what rate is available?” It is “what is the lender actually pricing, and why?”

At Emet Capital, we treat pricing as a structure issue before it becomes a number issue. Better security, cleaner documents, a stronger story, and lender fit can matter as much as the headline pricing itself.

## What commercial loan interest rates actually measure

Commercial loan interest rates are not just a public shelf price. They reflect how a lender views the overall risk, liquidity, and complexity of a business-purpose transaction. The same amount borrowed for two different reasons can attract very different pricing because the lender is not only funding the amount. It is funding the structure.

A lender usually asks questions such as:

- Is the loan secured or unsecured?
- What asset, property, or cash flow supports repayment?
- Is the transaction straightforward or deadline-driven?
- How strong is the borrower’s documentation?
- Does the loan solve a stable long-term need or a short-term pressure point?

For example, a well-documented commercial property acquisition will be assessed differently from a fast refinance of expensive short-term debt, even if both are described as commercial loans. That is why borrowers should compare offers inside the context of the actual deal, not just by keyword.

## What usually drives commercial loan pricing in Australia

Commercial loan pricing usually moves on a cluster of factors rather than one headline variable.

### Security and collateral quality

A lender prices secured debt differently from unsecured debt because security changes the recovery position if the deal goes wrong. Property-backed debt often prices differently from equipment-backed debt, and both differ again from unsecured business lending.

Within property-backed transactions, the security itself matters. A mainstream metro warehouse, a short-lease retail property, and a vacant mixed-use site may all support finance, but lender appetite will not be identical. That is why guides like [commercial property valuation for finance](/resources/guides/commercial-property-valuation-for-finance-lender-requirements) and [commercial property loan serviceability](/resources/guides/commercial-property-loan-serviceability-how-much-can-you-borrow) matter when comparing pricing.

### Cash flow and repayment strength

Some lenders care most about ongoing serviceability. Others are more focused on the strength of the security and the clarity of the exit. In either case, the borrower’s repayment profile matters.

Clean financials, stable revenue, sensible debt levels, and a clear business-purpose use of funds usually widen the lender pool. A messy file can still be fundable, but pricing often reflects that extra complexity.

### Loan purpose

Lenders price purchases, refinances, cash-out requests, restructures, and short-term bridge scenarios differently. A loan used to acquire a stable business asset is usually easier to underwrite than one used to solve an urgent timing problem.

That is why a borrower facing settlement pressure may first need something like [bridging finance in Australia](/resources/guides/bridging-finance-australia-complete-property-guide), then refinance later into a cleaner long-term facility once the pressure point is gone.

### Leverage and risk position

Higher leverage can increase lender caution because there is less equity buffer protecting the loan. Lower leverage does not always guarantee the cheapest offer, but it usually improves structure flexibility and the number of realistic lenders.

Borrowers often focus on the maximum advance. In practice, a slightly more conservative structure can create a materially stronger pricing and approval outcome.

### Time pressure and execution risk

When a transaction is urgent, certainty can matter more than rate. A lender willing to complete on a hard deadline is solving a different problem from a lender offering cleaner pricing on a slow-moving file.

That trade-off is common in [commercial property settlement](/resources/guides/commercial-property-settlement-process-finance-timeline), refinance maturities, and business restructuring situations.

## Bank rates versus non-bank and private pricing

Banks, non-bank lenders, and private lenders do not compete on identical terms because they usually solve different parts of the market.

Banks often suit cleaner files with stronger documentation, simpler security, and lower tolerance for ambiguity. Non-bank lenders can be more adaptable around policy edges. Private lenders may fit short-term, urgent, or more complex scenarios where speed or structure is the main issue.

That does not mean one category is always better. It means the cheapest-looking quote may not be the most realistic quote. If a lender is unlikely to finish valuation, legal, or credit in time, the headline price may not be the real offer at all.

## How to compare commercial loan offers properly

The best way to compare commercial loan offers is to compare the whole credit package.

### Look beyond the headline rate

A strong comparison should include:

- loan term
- repayment profile
- fees and line items
- covenants or review triggers
- valuation assumptions
- conditions precedent
- refinance or exit requirements
- likelihood of settlement on time

A cheaper headline can still be the weaker commercial outcome if the structure is brittle or the lender is a poor fit.

### Compare certainty as well as cost

A commercial borrower should always ask: can this lender actually execute this deal inside the required timeframe and policy envelope? If the answer is uncertain, the rate alone is not the decision.

That issue becomes even more important in refinance scenarios, where a borrower may need certainty first and optimisation second. Our [commercial property refinancing solutions guide](/resources/guides/commercial-property-refinancing-solutions) explains that trade-off in more detail.

## When pricing is not the real problem

Sometimes borrowers focus on rates because rates are easy to ask about. The real blocker is often something else: short lease risk, poor valuation support, weak documents, unclear use of funds, or a structure that does not match the lender’s appetite.

In those cases, improving the file can matter more than shopping harder. A clearer entity chart, better lease evidence, stronger explanation of use of funds, or a more realistic leverage request can materially improve lender engagement.

A clean quotable way to think about it is this: commercial loan interest rates are the visible output of lender confidence. If lender confidence is weak, headline pricing usually gets worse or disappears entirely.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What affects commercial loan interest rates in Australia?

Commercial loan interest rates in Australia are usually influenced by the loan purpose, whether the debt is secured or unsecured, the quality of the security, the borrower’s financial profile, leverage, and how straightforward the transaction is to complete. Lenders are pricing the whole risk profile rather than just the amount requested.

### Are commercial loan rates the same for all business borrowers?

No. Two business borrowers can receive different pricing even if they request similar amounts because lenders assess industry risk, document quality, security strength, and repayment certainty differently. Commercial pricing is highly scenario-dependent.

### Do banks always offer better commercial loan pricing than private lenders?

Not always. Banks may suit cleaner, lower-risk files, but private or non-bank lenders can be the better commercial fit when a deal has timing pressure, policy complexity, or a short-term transition need. The better offer is the one that both fits the deal and has a realistic path to settlement.

### Should I compare only the interest rate on a commercial loan?

No. A commercial borrower should compare the full structure of the loan, including fees, term, repayment profile, covenants, conditions, and execution certainty. A low headline rate can still produce a poor outcome if the structure is restrictive or the lender cannot complete.

### Can refinancing improve my commercial loan pricing?

Potentially, yes. Refinancing can improve pricing when the borrower is better documented, the security has strengthened, the leverage has improved, or the original loan was written as a short-term or specialist solution. The new lender still needs to see a cleaner overall file.

### When should a business borrower prioritise certainty over rate?

A business borrower should usually prioritise certainty over rate when there is a hard settlement date, a lender maturity deadline, or another time-critical event that could create larger downstream costs if the transaction fails. In those cases, the cheapest quote is not always the strongest commercial option.
