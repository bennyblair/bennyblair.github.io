# Commercial Real Estate Lenders in Australia

Commercial real estate lenders in Australia are the banks, non-banks, specialist credit providers, and private lenders that fund business-purpose property transactions. That can include offices, warehouses, retail premises, mixed-use assets, industrial property, medical suites, development sites, and other income-producing or business-use real estate. The important point is that these lenders do not all operate the same way. They differ on speed, leverage, property preference, documentation depth, and how much complexity they will tolerate.

For borrowers, the mistake is often thinking the market is just one big lender list. In reality, commercial real estate lending is a segmented market. A major bank may suit a clean owner-occupied warehouse purchase. A non-bank lender may suit a short-WALE mixed-use refinance. A private lender may suit a short-term bridge or a deal with urgency that mainstream credit cannot meet in time. So the better question is not "Who are the lenders?" It is "Which lender category fits this asset, this timeline, and this commercial objective?"

This guide explains how commercial real estate lenders in Australia differ, what they usually care about, where borrower fit matters most, and how to compare the market without reducing everything to rate-shopping. ## What counts as commercial real estate lending?

Commercial real estate lending usually refers to funding secured by business-purpose real estate. That can include:

- office properties
- industrial and logistics assets
- retail premises and strata retail
- mixed-use assets
- medical, allied health, or childcare property
- commercial development or transitional property
- owner-occupied business premises

The lending purpose may be acquisition, refinance, equity release, development, bridging, or business restructuring linked to property security.

## The main types of commercial real estate lenders in Australia

### Major banks

Major banks generally suit cleaner, lower-complexity commercial real estate transactions. They tend to prefer standard security, clearer servicing, straightforward entity structures, and enough time for formal credit, valuation, and legal processes.

Where the property and borrower profile are strong, banks can be a logical first stop for longer-term debt.

### Regional and second-tier banks

Regional and second-tier lenders can sometimes be more flexible in selected sectors or locations. They still operate within structured credit frameworks, but their appetite may differ from the majors depending on the market and asset type.

### Non-bank commercial real estate lenders

Non-bank lenders often work well when a transaction is credible but not especially bank-friendly. That could mean a tighter deadline, a more specialised property, uneven lease profile, recent credit friction, or a structure that needs more commercial judgment.

Some non-banks focus on mainstream property-backed lending. Others specialise in refinance transitions, construction-adjacent debt, residual stock, or higher-complexity scenarios.

### Private lenders and private credit funds

Private lenders usually sit further along the flexibility spectrum. They may be relevant where speed, bespoke structure, unusual collateral, or short-term problem-solving matter more than conventional policy fit.

That does not mean private lenders are casual about risk. It means they may assess the transaction differently, often with stronger focus on security, leverage control, and the defined exit.

## What commercial real estate lenders usually assess

### Asset quality

The property itself usually comes first. Lenders look at location, use, building quality, marketability, tenancy profile, title issues, and how easy the asset would be to understand and value.

### Lease strength and income profile

For investment property, lease quality can be central. Tenant covenant, lease expiry profile, vacancy risk, and rental durability often shape lender appetite.

If the lease profile is weak or short, the funding path may move away from standard bank credit and closer to a specialist lender conversation.

### Borrower strength and structure

Lenders also assess who is borrowing, how the asset is held, whether guarantees are needed, and whether the overall entity structure is straightforward enough to document and settle efficiently.

### Leverage and equity buffer

A workable transaction is not just about how much you want to borrow. It is about whether the requested leverage still leaves the lender comfortable if valuation, leasing, or market conditions soften.

### Purpose and exit

Lenders want to understand what the money is for and how the debt is expected to behave. A long-term owner-occupied mortgage is a different credit story from a short-term bridge while another sale or refinance catches up.

## Why lender fit matters more than just lender names

Two lenders can look similar from the outside and still behave very differently once the deal is submitted.

One may like metro industrial. Another may dislike short remaining lease terms. Another may be comfortable with owner-occupied commercial property but not mixed-use assets. Another may be fine with refinance pressure but only if the exit is clearly defined.

That is why the borrower goal should be to find the right lender category, not just a longer spreadsheet.

## When a bank may be the right fit

A bank may make sense when:

- the property is mainstream and easy to value
- the borrower has strong financials and a clean servicing story
- there is enough time for full valuation and legal review
- the transaction is designed as longer-term debt
- the structure is straightforward and well documented

## When a non-bank lender may be the right fit

A non-bank lender may be more realistic when:

- settlement timing is compressed
- the property sits near the edge of bank policy
- the lease profile is weaker or more complicated
- the borrower structure is layered but still workable
- the file is fundamentally sound, just not neatly bank-shaped

## When a private lender may be relevant

A private lender may be relevant when:

- the issue is short-term timing rather than long-term debt
- the borrower needs a bespoke structure
- an existing lender maturity is creating real pressure
- there is a clear refinance, sale, or project exit but it is not ready yet
- conventional credit cannot move fast enough for the commercial deadline

In those situations, the better comparison is often between a short-term specialist structure and the cost of missing the transaction entirely.

## When not to force the wrong lender path

Sometimes borrowers spend too long trying to make a mainstream lender say yes to a file that is obviously transitional, urgent, or structurally awkward.

That can waste time and narrow options. If the commercial real estate deal clearly has a short-term problem, it is usually better to treat it as a short-term problem early.

The opposite is also true. If the transaction is simple, well supported, and long-term by nature, overcomplicating it with specialist debt can add unnecessary friction.

## Common borrower scenarios

### Scenario 1: Owner-occupied industrial acquisition

A business owner buying an established warehouse with strong trading history and reasonable settlement time will often fit best with a mainstream bank or second-tier lender. The property is understandable, the use is clear, and the debt objective is long term.

### Scenario 2: Mixed-use investment refinance with a short lease issue

An investor refinancing a mixed-use asset with uneven lease profile may find mainstream lenders cautious, especially if the existing lender is already at maturity. That file may sit better with a non-bank or specialist lender first, then be refinanced later once the tenancy story improves.

### Scenario 3: Time-sensitive commercial acquisition

A borrower exchanging on a short-settlement commercial asset may not have enough time for standard bank execution even if the deal is otherwise good. In that case, specialist or short-term funding may simply be the appropriate capital source.

### Scenario 4: Development transition or residual position

Developers sometimes need interim debt between one stage and the next. When titles, stock sales, refinance approval, or project milestones are still in motion, lender fit becomes more about execution and exit than about headline product labels.

## How to compare commercial real estate lenders properly

A better comparison framework is:

1. Which lender category actually suits this transaction?
2. How comfortable is that lender with this exact property type?
3. How much time does the process realistically need?
4. What happens if the valuation or legal review takes longer than expected?
5. Is the proposed debt long-term, transitional, or bridge-like by design?
6. Does the lender's appetite match the borrower's real objective?

That gives you a much more useful answer than asking who looks cheapest on day one.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is a commercial real estate lender?

A commercial real estate lender is a bank, non-bank lender, private lender, or specialist fund that provides finance secured by business-purpose property. These lenders may fund acquisitions, refinances, equity release, development transitions, or short-term commercial property transactions.

### Are banks the main commercial real estate lenders in Australia?

Banks are a major part of the market, but they are not the whole market. Non-bank lenders and private credit providers also play an important role, especially where timing, structure, or policy complexity makes mainstream bank credit harder to use.

### What is the difference between a non-bank and a private commercial real estate lender?

A non-bank lender is usually an institutional or specialist lender outside the deposit-taking banking system, while a private lender may be a private credit fund, family office, or other bespoke capital provider. In practice, the difference that matters most is how each lender approaches risk, speed, and structure.

### How do commercial real estate lenders assess risk?

Most assess the property quality, lease profile, borrower structure, leverage, purpose of funds, and expected exit. Different lender categories place different weight on each factor depending on the type of transaction.

### When might a private lender be used for commercial real estate?

A private lender may be used when the transaction is short term, time-sensitive, or too bespoke for mainstream bank policy. That can include bridge scenarios, maturing debt, unusual structures, or transitional property situations with a defined exit.

### Should I compare commercial real estate lenders only by pricing?

No. Pricing matters, but so do structure, speed, legal complexity, conditions, and lender fit. A lower-cost lender that cannot execute on the required timeline may not be the best real-world option.

## Bottom line

Commercial real estate lenders in Australia do not all solve the same problem.

The most useful approach is to understand what kind of debt the transaction actually needs, then work backward toward the lender category most likely to execute cleanly. That may be a bank. It may be a non-bank. It may be private capital for a short period before the permanent solution is ready. The point is not to chase a bigger list. It is to match the property, the timeline, and the commercial objective to the right part of the market.
