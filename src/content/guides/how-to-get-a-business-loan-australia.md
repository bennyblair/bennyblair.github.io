# How to Get a Business Loan in Australia

Getting a business loan in Australia usually means showing a lender what the money is for, how the business will repay it, what security or support is available, and why the facility fits the commercial purpose. A strong application is not just a form. It is a clear borrower story supported by documents, cash-flow evidence, and a realistic exit plan.

For business owners, property investors, and developers, the fastest path is to prepare the file before approaching lenders. That means matching the loan type to the need, checking whether the business can service the facility, and understanding whether a bank, non-bank lender, private lender, or specialist commercial finance provider is likely to assess the scenario.

This guide explains how to get a business loan in Australia from a commercial borrower perspective. It covers preparation, lender assessment, documents, timing, security, and the common mistakes that slow approvals.

## Who This Is For

This guide is for Australian business owners, directors, property investors, developers, and commercial borrowers preparing for business finance.

It is not written for consumer credit, personal loans, retail borrowing, or owner-occupier home loans. It is general information for commercial borrowers only.

## Step 1: Define the Loan Purpose

A lender wants to know exactly why the business needs the money. A clear purpose helps the lender decide whether the loan type, term, security, and repayment structure make sense.

Common commercial purposes include working capital, supplier payments, BAS or tax timing, stock purchases, equipment, fitout, business acquisition, property settlement, refinancing, and bridging a temporary gap.

The purpose should be specific. "Cash flow" is often too broad. "Funding a supplier deposit while waiting for approved invoices to be paid" gives a lender more context and makes it easier to match the file to a suitable structure.

If the funding need is short term, compare it with [short-term business finance](/resources/guides/short-term-business-finance-australia) and [working capital loans](/resources/guides/working-capital-loans-for-smes) before assuming a standard term loan is the best fit.

## Step 2: Work Out the Amount and Timing

A business loan request should be large enough to solve the problem, but not larger than the business can justify or manage. Lenders will usually ask how the requested amount was calculated.

A practical funding calculation might include the main invoice, taxes, settlement adjustments, legal costs, valuation costs, lender fees, working capital buffer, and any refinance payout figure.

Timing matters as much as amount. If funds are needed for a hard settlement date, auction, supplier deadline, or refinance expiry, the lender must know that early. A slower bank process may not suit a time-critical file, while faster lenders may require stronger security or a clearer exit.

For property-backed time pressure, the lender may compare structures such as [bridging finance](/resources/guides/bridging-finance-australia-complete-property-guide), [second mortgages](/resources/guides/second-mortgages-for-business-guide), or private lending.

## Step 3: Choose the Right Type of Business Loan

The right business loan depends on purpose, term, security, repayment source, and urgency. A fixed business loan may suit a defined purchase or refinance. A line of credit may suit recurring working capital. Invoice finance may suit debtor timing. Asset finance may suit equipment.

A borrower should not start with the lender's product name. Start with the commercial problem. Is the business funding growth, covering a short timing gap, replacing expensive debt, buying an asset, or protecting a settlement?

A [business line of credit](/resources/guides/business-line-of-credit-australia) may be better where funds are drawn and repaid repeatedly. [Invoice finance](/resources/guides/invoice-finance-australia-complete-guide) may be relevant when unpaid invoices are the main issue. [Asset-backed lending](/resources/guides/asset-backed-lending-and-asset-finance) may fit when equipment, receivables, or other assets support the facility.

## Step 4: Prepare the Documents

Complete documents make a business loan easier to assess. Missing documents are one of the most common reasons a file stalls.

Most commercial lenders may ask for:

- recent business financial statements or management accounts
- BAS and tax lodgement history
- business bank statements
- ATO statement or payment arrangement details, if relevant
- existing loan statements
- company, trust, and director information
- property details, rates notices, leases, or valuations if property-backed
- invoices, contracts, purchase orders, or settlement statements supporting the loan purpose
- asset lists or equipment details where asset security is relevant

The document set changes by lender and loan type. A mainstream bank may require deeper servicing evidence. A private lender may focus more heavily on security, equity, borrower conduct, and exit strategy. For a broader checklist, see [business loan requirements](/resources/guides/business-loan-requirements-australia-commercial-borrowers).

## Step 5: Understand Serviceability

Serviceability means the business can meet the repayment obligations under the proposed facility. Lenders assess this differently, but the underlying question is simple: can the borrower afford the debt without creating a new problem?

A trading business may be assessed through profit, cash flow, bank statement conduct, debtor receipts, contracts, and tax position. A property-backed borrower may also be assessed through rental income, asset value, loan-to-value ratio, and refinance or sale exit.

The borrower should test the facility against a downside scenario. What happens if sales are delayed, a debtor pays late, a tenant leaves, or a refinance takes longer than expected?

If several debts are already creating pressure, [business debt consolidation](/resources/guides/business-debt-consolidation-australia) may be relevant, but consolidation only helps when the new structure is sustainable.

## Step 6: Consider Security and Guarantees

Security can change the lender pool, loan amount, pricing, and speed. Commercial security may include property, equipment, receivables, business assets, registered charges, guarantees, or related-party support.

Property-backed loans can include commercial property, investment property, development sites, or second-ranking security. A borrower comparing property-backed structures should understand [commercial property loans](/resources/guides/commercial-property-loans-australia-complete-guide), [second mortgages for business](/resources/guides/second-mortgages-for-business-guide), and [caveat loans](/resources/guides/caveat-loans-australia-complete-guide).

Guarantees also matter. A company borrower may still create personal obligations for directors or guarantors if the facility includes guarantees or indemnities. Borrowers should obtain legal, accounting, or financial advice where appropriate before committing.

## Step 7: Match the Lender to the File

Not every lender wants every business loan. The same borrower may receive different outcomes depending on the lender's risk appetite, security preference, industry view, documentation standard, and timing requirements.

Banks may suit lower-urgency, well-documented files with strong servicing and clean conduct. Non-bank lenders may suit commercial scenarios that need more flexibility. Private lenders may suit eligible business borrowers where speed, security, and exit strategy are more important than a standard bank process.

The aim is not simply to find a lender that says yes. The aim is to find a structure the business can understand, manage, and exit. [Private lending vs bank lending](/resources/guides/private-lending-vs-bank-lending-which-is-better) explains the practical differences.

## When To Use a Business Loan

A business loan may be appropriate when the funding purpose is commercial, the repayment source is credible, and the loan improves the business position rather than delaying a deeper issue.

Common good-fit scenarios include buying stock for confirmed demand, funding equipment that supports revenue, bridging receivables, refinancing a facility before expiry, completing a settlement, or restructuring debt into a more manageable shape.

The file is stronger when the borrower can explain the problem, the amount, the timing, the documents, and the exit in plain language.

## When Not To Use a Business Loan

A business loan is usually a poor fit when there is no clear repayment source, no operational plan, poor document readiness, or ongoing losses that the loan will not fix.

It may also be unsuitable where the requested term is shorter than the expected cash event, where the business is using debt to pay debt repeatedly, or where security providers do not understand the risk.

If the business is under severe creditor, tax, or legal pressure, it may need accounting, legal, or restructuring advice before taking on new debt.

## Application Checklist

Before approaching lenders, prepare a short summary that answers:

1. What does the business do?
2. How much is required?
3. What is the exact purpose?
4. When are funds needed?
5. How will the loan be repaid?
6. What security is available?
7. What documents support the request?
8. What could go wrong, and what is the contingency plan?

A clean summary can save days of back-and-forth. It also helps a broker identify which lenders are realistic before the application is submitted.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do you get a business loan in Australia?

You get a business loan in Australia by defining the commercial purpose, preparing financial and supporting documents, identifying the repayment source, checking available security, and applying to a lender whose policy fits the scenario. Strong applications explain the amount, timing, use of funds, and exit clearly.

### What documents are needed for a business loan?

Common documents include financial statements, BAS, bank statements, ATO position, existing loan statements, company or trust details, property information, invoices, contracts, and evidence supporting the loan purpose. Requirements vary by lender, loan type, security, and urgency.

### Can a new business get a business loan?

A new business may be able to access business finance, but assessment is usually harder because there is less trading history. Lenders may place more weight on security, director experience, contracts, cash-flow evidence, asset backing, or a clear commercial exit.

### Is a secured business loan easier to get?

A secured business loan may be easier to assess where the security is suitable and the repayment plan is credible. Security can improve lender appetite, but it does not remove the need for a commercial purpose, borrower conduct, documents, and exit strategy.

### How long does a business loan application take?

Timing depends on the lender, documentation, security, valuation, legal work, and complexity. A complete, well-matched commercial file can move faster than an incomplete file sent to the wrong lender. Hard deadlines should be disclosed at the start.

### What is the biggest mistake when applying for a business loan?

The biggest mistake is applying before the borrower can explain the purpose, repayment source, security position, and documents. A vague or incomplete application can waste time and make the file harder to place with suitable lenders.
