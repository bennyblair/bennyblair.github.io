Bridging finance provides essential short-term funding solutions enabling property developers navigating complex financing transitions between project stages, site acquisitions, or permanent funding arrangements. Australian property development projects rarely proceed smoothly from land purchase through construction completion with single continuous financing facility, instead involving multiple funding phases requiring strategic capital deployment matching specific project milestones, risk profiles, and lender requirements. Developers increasingly utilise bridging finance addressing timing mismatches including site acquisition before construction finance approval, initial works funding enabling construction loan conditions satisfaction, or project completion gaps between construction loan expiry and sales settlement proceeds.

Development project complexity creates numerous scenarios where conventional construction finance proves inadequate or unavailable, driving developers toward bridging solutions delivering faster approval, more flexible lending criteria, and customised terms matching unique project circumstances. Land acquisition opportunities requiring immediate unconditional offers and rapid settlement suit bridging finance enabling site securing whilst progressing construction finance applications spanning 8-16 weeks approval timelines. Pre-development expenditure including demolition, remediation, service connections, or preliminary works often falls outside construction finance scope requiring separate funding bridges until main construction commences. Project extensions where construction overruns exhaust approved loan terms before completion necessitate extension bridges maintaining funding through final stages preventing disastrous mid-project financing failures.

## Understanding Developer Bridging Finance Fundamentals

Developer bridging finance differs substantially from residential bridging supporting homeowners upgrading properties or property investors managing settlement timing gaps. Development bridges accommodate significantly larger loan sizes typically ranging $500,000 to $20 million+ compared to residential bridges of $200,000 to $2 million, reflecting development project capital intensity requiring substantial land costs plus preliminary works funding. Terms typically span 6-24 months versus residential bridges at 6-12 months, providing extended timeframes enabling lengthy development approval processes, construction finance applications, or initial construction phases preceding project completion and sales proceeds. Interest rates range 9-16% annually depending on loan-to-value ratios, developer experience, and project risk profiles, sitting 2-4% above residential bridge equivalents reflecting elevated development risks from market volatility, construction complications, or approval uncertainties.

Security structures emphasise development site value plus potentially additional properties developers contribute strengthening lender security positions. First mortgage security against development sites provides primary protection, with lenders typically advancing 60-75% of site value depending on location, zoning, and development potential. Experienced developers with proven track records and strong financial positions sometimes secure 70-75% loan-to-value ratios, whilst newer developers or higher-risk projects face conservative 55-65% LVRs requiring larger equity contributions. Additional security from developers' other properties including residential holdings, commercial properties, or completed investment projects enables higher borrowing potentially reaching 70-80% of combined security values when lenders aggregate multiple properties' equity supporting single development project funding.

Approval criteria assess developer capability, project viability, and exit strategy certainty rather than detailed income verification conventional lending emphasises. Lenders evaluate development experience through previous project reviews examining completed developments, outcomes achieved, and any problem projects revealing capability gaps. Track record assessment proves particularly important for larger or complex projects, with lenders typically requiring minimum 2-3 successfully completed similar-scale projects before advancing significant bridge funding. Project viability analysis examines development approvals status, preliminary cost estimates, market absorption analysis supporting sales assumptions, and feasibility modelling demonstrating adequate profit margins covering contingencies and generating acceptable developer returns justifying project pursuit.

Exit strategy clarity determines approval confidence, with defined repayment paths from construction finance refinancing, presale settlements, or alternative property sales providing lenders comfort around bridge repayment within proposed terms. Construction finance pre-approval from recognised lenders strengthens applications, demonstrating viable refinancing exit enabling bridge repayment upon construction loan settlement typically 3-6 months following bridge drawdown. Presale contracts achieving minimum thresholds of 30-50% project value provide alternative exits through settlement proceeds, particularly for townhouse or apartment developments with stage release strategies enabling progressive sales settlements funding bridge repayment before full project completion. Some developers propose exit through unrelated property sales, though lenders scrutinise these approaches ensuring genuine market availability rather than optimistic assumptions about achieving sales within tight bridge terms.

## Securing Site Acquisition Bridging Finance

Land acquisition timing frequently creates financing gaps where developers identify attractive sites requiring immediate purchase commitment but lack available capital or approved construction finance enabling settlement. Competitive land markets particularly in metropolitan growth corridors or established inner-city areas often generate multiple buyer interest, with vendors favouring unconditional offers or short settlement periods of 30-60 days over extended timelines enabling conventional construction finance approval. Bridging finance enables developers making competitive offers with standard settlement terms whilst progressing construction finance applications in parallel, preventing lost acquisition opportunities from extended approval processes potentially spanning 12-20+ weeks between site identification and construction loan settlement.

Site acquisition bridges typically advance 60-70% of land purchase price, requiring developers contributing 30-40% equity from personal resources, investor capital, or profits from previous projects. On $2 million development site, typical bridge provides $1.2-1.4 million with developers funding $600,000-800,000 deposit and settlement balance. This structure ensures developers maintain meaningful equity stakes aligning interests with lenders whilst demonstrating financial capacity managing development risks. Some lenders advance higher 70-75% LVRs for premium sites in proven locations with strong development fundamentals including excellent transport access, established infrastructure, supportive planning frameworks, and demonstrated buyer demand from comparable recent developments.

Approval timelines for acquisition bridges span 5-10 business days from complete application to settlement, substantially faster than construction finance requiring 8-16 weeks comprehensive assessment. Expedited approval derives from simplified assessment focusing primarily on site value, purchase price reasonableness, developer equity contribution adequacy, and credible exit strategy rather than detailed feasibility analysis or comprehensive project assessment construction lenders conduct. Essential documentation includes site purchase contract, preliminary site valuation or comparable sales evidence supporting purchase price, developer identification and entity documentation, evidence of deposit payment, and construction finance application confirmation with intended permanent lender demonstrating realistic exit strategy.

Interest capitalisation structures prove standard for acquisition bridges, eliminating monthly payment obligations whilst accumulating interest added to loan principal monthly. This approach preserves developer cash flow during planning approval, design development, and construction finance application phases where projects generate no income but require ongoing expenditure on consultants, planning submissions, and project development activities. On $1.5 million acquisition bridge at 12% annually, monthly capitalised interest totals $15,000 accumulating to $90,000-180,000 over typical 6-12 month bridge terms before construction finance refinancing. Total bridging costs including establishment fees of 1-2% plus capitalised interest typically reach 8-15% of bridge amount depending on term duration and rate negotiations—material expense warranting careful exit timeline planning preventing extended bridge periods unnecessarily accumulating interest costs eroding development profit margins.

## Funding Pre-Development Works and Approvals

Pre-development funding bridges support expenditure between site acquisition and construction commencement, addressing financing gaps where developers require capital for demolition, contamination remediation, service connections, detailed design completion, or planning application costs before construction finance approvals enable formal project drawdown. [Construction finance](/resources/guides/construction-finance-australia-complete-guide) facilities typically provide funding only after satisfying numerous conditions including final development approval, detailed building plans, fixed-price builder contracts, minimum presale requirements, and potentially other conditions varying by lender and project complexity. Satisfying these conditions requires significant pre-development expenditure of $50,000-500,000+ depending on project scale and site complexity that conventional construction loans exclude from funding scope.

Demolition and remediation bridges enable removal of existing structures or contamination treatment required before construction commencement, with costs potentially reaching $100,000-$1 million+ for substantial buildings or contaminated industrial sites. Construction lenders generally exclude demolition and remediation from construction facility scope, viewing these as pre-commencement activities falling outside building construction funding parameters. Specialist demolition and remediation bridges advance 70-80% of verified costs based on fixed-price contractor quotations, with developers contributing 20-30% equity ensuring cost control alignment. These bridges typically span 3-6 months covering demolition works completion periods, with exit through construction loan initial drawdown refinancing bridge amounts once construction formally commences following site clearing completion.

Planning approval pursuit funding supports consultant fees for town planners, architects, engineers, environmental specialists, traffic consultants, and other experts required preparing comprehensive development applications for local councils or planning authorities. Major development applications for multi-unit residential, commercial, or mixed-use projects generate consultant costs of $50,000-300,000 requiring 6-18 months approval processes before receiving development permits enabling construction finance applications. Planning approval bridges advance against developer equity in projects plus potentially future development site value increases from approval obtainment transforming raw land into development-ready sites commanding 20-50% value premiums over undeveloped equivalents. Exit occurs through construction finance refinancing or potentially site sales if developers pursue land banking strategies involving approval obtainment then selling permitted sites to builder-developers or institutions.

Service connection funding bridges capital required bringing electricity, water, sewer, gas, and telecommunications infrastructure to development sites, particularly greenfield locations requiring extensive new infrastructure installation. Service authority charges potentially total $50,000-500,000+ depending on site location, development scale, and required infrastructure upgrades to existing networks accommodating additional development capacity. These charges typically require payment before construction commencement or during early project stages, falling outside construction finance initial drawdown provisions focused on building construction costs rather than pre-commencement infrastructure. Service connection bridges provide short 3-9 month funding with exit through construction loan drawdowns once formal building construction commences enabling infrastructure costs inclusion in total project funding refinancing pre-development bridges.

## Bridging Construction Finance Transitions

Construction completion bridges address timing gaps between construction loan expiry and project sales settlement, supporting developers completing final works, obtaining occupation certificates, and achieving sales settlements generating exit proceeds. Construction finance typically provides 12-24 month terms for residential development projects based on projected construction timelines, with fixed expiry dates requiring loan repayment regardless of construction status at term conclusion. Construction delays from weather events, builder complications, approval holdups, or material shortages frequently extend actual completion beyond originally projected timelines, creating situations where construction loans approach expiry before projects reach practical completion enabling sales settlements.

Extension bridges refinancing maturing construction loans provide 6-12 month additional terms enabling construction completion, defect rectification, and sales campaign execution achieving settlement proceeds repaying extension bridges. These bridges prove essential preventing disastrous scenarios where construction lenders demand repayment on maturity dates despite incomplete projects generating no sales proceeds, potentially forcing mortgagee sales at substantial discounts destroying developer equity. Extension bridge amounts typically match outstanding construction loan balances plus accrued interest, capitalised establishment costs, and potentially small additional amounts funding final works completion. Interest rates of 11-16% annually reflect elevated risk from delayed projects suggesting complications, with lenders scrutinising completion estimates and remaining works costs ensuring adequate timeframes and funding completing projects.

Sales settlement timing bridges address gaps where completed projects achieve sales contracts but settlements extend 30-90 days beyond construction loan expiry, with construction lenders unwilling extending terms accommodating settlement timing requiring separate bridge funding. Off-the-plan apartment or townhouse sales commonly involve 90-day settlement periods from contract execution, potentially extending beyond construction loan terms if contracts occur near loan expiry dates. Settlement bridges refinance construction loans providing 3-6 month terms specifically timed to settlement dates specified in sales contracts, with repayment from settlement proceeds typically involving solicitors coordinating bridge discharge simultaneously with sales settlement fund receipt.

Cost-overrun bridges supplement exhausted construction budgets where unexpected costs or scope changes exceed approved construction loan amounts, requiring additional capital completing projects without mid-construction funding failures. Construction cost variations potentially total 10-30% above original estimates from design changes, unforeseen site conditions, material price escalation, or builder variations for additional works. Construction lenders rarely increase approved loan amounts mid-project, maintaining conservative positions around initial feasibility assessments and refusing reactive funding for budget overruns potentially signalling poor project management or unrealistic initial planning. Cost-overrun bridges typically advance 60-70% of additional required costs with developers contributing remaining 30-40% equity, structured as second mortgages behind construction first mortgages with repayment from sales proceeds after first mortgage discharge.

## Managing Bridging Finance Costs and Structures

Interest rate negotiation opportunities exist across development bridging finance market with rates varying 3-5% between conservative institutional lenders and aggressive private operators for comparable scenarios. Experienced developers with strong track records, substantial equity contributions, and premium development sites in proven locations secure rates at lower spectrum around 9-11% annually, whilst newer developers, higher-risk projects, or subordinated second mortgage positions face rates of 13-16% reflecting elevated lender risk perceptions. Engaging mortgage brokers specialising in development finance provides access to multiple lender options, enabling comparison across 5-10 potential lenders identifying most competitive pricing and favourable terms for specific project characteristics rather than accepting first available quote potentially overpricing by 2-4% annually.

Establishment fees of 1-2% of loan amounts plus legal fees of $2,000-8,000, valuation fees of $1,500-5,000, and potentially other charges including facility fees or line fees add to total bridging costs requiring careful budgeting. On $2 million development bridge at 12% annually for 12 months with 1.5% establishment fee, total costs reach approximately $270,000 comprising $240,000 interest plus $30,000 establishment fee—material expense requiring inclusion in development feasibility analysis ensuring adequate profit margins accommodate bridging costs without destroying project viability. Lower establishment fees sometimes correlate with higher interest rates as lenders structure pricing differently across upfront and ongoing components, warranting total cost comparison across anticipated bridge term rather than focusing exclusively on headline interest rates potentially misleading when establishment fee variations substantially alter total expenses.

Interest payment versus capitalisation structures significantly impact cash flow requirements and total interest costs, with monthly payment structures requiring developers funding approximately 0.75-1.3% of loan amount monthly from personal resources or project cash flows. On $2 million bridge at 12% annually, monthly interest payments total $20,000—substantial cash outflow potentially straining developer resources during pre-revenue project phases. Interest capitalisation eliminates ongoing payments through monthly interest addition to principal, increasing effective rates through compounding whilst preserving cash flow enabling investment in development progression. Total capitalised interest costs exceed payment equivalent by approximately 5-8% over 12-month terms from compounding effect, though cash preservation benefits often justify marginally higher costs particularly for developers managing multiple concurrent projects requiring working capital flexibility.

[Commercial property development finance](/resources/guides/commercial-property-development-finance) involving bridging components often involves mixed structures combining interest capitalisation during initial pre-development or construction phases transitioning to interest payments during sales or stabilisation periods when projects generate revenue from presale deposits or completed leasing. This hybrid approach optimises cash flow management whilst controlling total interest costs, capitalising during cash-negative phases whilst requiring payments once projects achieve positive cash generation capability. Negotiating capitalisation limits preventing unlimited accumulation protects both developers and lenders from excessive interest burden potentially exceeding original loan amounts if extended delays occur, with typical caps at 120-150% original principal requiring refinancing or restructuring if limits approach.

## Planning Strategic Exit Strategies and Refinancing

Construction finance refinancing represents primary exit strategy for acquisition and pre-development bridges, with construction loan settlements discharging bridge facilities typically 6-12 months following bridge drawdown. Successful construction finance exits require progressing formal applications during bridge terms, satisfying all lender conditions including development approvals, detailed plans, builder contracts, and presale minimums, and coordinating settlement timing ensuring bridge discharge before term expiry avoiding expensive extensions. Developers should secure construction finance pre-approval before accessing bridge funding, providing lenders confidence around viable exit strategy whilst enabling accelerated construction loan progression during bridge terms focusing on condition satisfaction rather than initial application assessment.

Presale settlement exits suit residential development projects generating early sales revenue enabling bridge repayment without construction loan refinancing, particularly for townhouse or house-and-land developments with stage release strategies. Achieving 30-50% presale contracts during bridge terms creates exit path through settlement proceeds, though requires active sales campaign prosecution during bridge periods and realistic pricing attracting buyer interest in competitive markets. Settlement timing coordination proves critical, as off-the-plan contracts typically involve 60-90 day settlement periods potentially extending beyond bridge expiry requiring either bridge extension negotiation or temporary alternative funding maintaining bridge current until settlement proceeds arrive.

Alternative property sales provide fallback exits where developers own unrelated investment properties, development sites, or residential holdings available for sale generating exit proceeds. This strategy suits established developers with diversified property portfolios enabling liquidity access through strategic asset sales if primary exit strategies encounter delays or complications. However, reliance on property sales carries risks from market condition deterioration potentially preventing sales at acceptable prices within bridge timeframes, warranting conservative valuation assumptions and adequate time buffers preventing forced distressed sales destroying value from urgent liquidation needs. Lenders scrutinise alternative sale exits more heavily than construction finance refinancing, requiring evidence of genuine market demand, realistic pricing, and potentially active marketing commencement during bridge terms confirming viable exit execution.

Extension negotiation strategies address scenarios where primary exits encounter delays requiring additional time completing projects, achieving sales, or securing refinancing. Proactive extension requests initiated 30-60 days before bridge expiry demonstrate professional developer communication enabling collaborative lender response, versus reactive requests days before expiry suggesting poor planning potentially triggering default procedures rather than cooperative extensions. Extension terms typically add 3-6 months to original bridge duration, though lenders charge extension fees of 0.5-1.5% plus potentially increased interest rates of 1-3% annually reflecting extended risk exposure from projects requiring extensions suggesting complications or challenges. Multiple extensions prove progressively harder securing as lenders lose confidence in developer capability or project viability, warranting realistic initial timeline estimation preventing reliance on extensions eroding lender relationships and increasing total project costs through accumulated fees and premium extension rates.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much can developers borrow through bridging finance?

Developer bridging finance loan amounts typically range $500,000 to $20 million+ depending on development site values, project scale, and developer equity contributions. Lenders advance 60-75% loan-to-value ratios against development site values for acquisition bridges, with experienced developers achieving higher 70-75% LVRs whilst newer developers face conservative 60-65% limits. On $3 million development site, typical bridge provides $1.8-2.25 million requiring developer equity of $750,000-$1.2 million. Pre-development and construction transition bridges may exceed site values when including completed works value, potentially reaching 70-80% of total as-is value for partially completed projects. Maximum borrowing depends on combined security values if developers pledge additional properties alongside development sites, potentially enabling larger loans through aggregated equity across multiple property holdings.

### What interest rate should developers expect on bridging finance?

Developer bridging finance interest rates typically range 9-16% annually depending on loan-to-value ratios, developer experience, project risk profiles, and security strength. Experienced developers with proven track records developing similar projects secure rates around 9-11% for first mortgage positions on premium sites with strong exit strategies, whilst newer developers or higher-risk projects face rates of 12-14%. Second mortgage positions behind construction loans command premium rates of 13-16% reflecting subordinated security. Regional variations exist, with metropolitan development sites in established markets achieving better rates than regional locations with limited comparable sales evidence or uncertain demand. Total borrowing costs including establishment fees, legal expenses, and capitalised interest typically reach 12-20% of bridge amounts over 12-month terms, warranting inclusion in development feasibility as material project cost component affecting profit margins.

### How long does bridging finance approval take for developers?

Developer bridging finance approval typically requires 5-10 business days from complete documentation submission to formal approval, substantially faster than construction finance requiring 8-16 weeks comprehensive assessment. Site acquisition bridges potentially achieve 3-5 day approvals for urgent requirements where developers face tight settlement deadlines, whilst pre-development or construction transition bridges allow standard 7-10 day timelines enabling thorough due diligence. Settlement following approval requires additional 5-7 days for legal documentation execution and security registration, creating total timelines of 10-17 days from application to fund receipt. Essential documentation includes development site contract or current title details, preliminary valuation or comparable sales evidence, development approval status confirmation, developer entity documents and identification, evidence of equity contribution sources, and exit strategy documentation including construction finance pre-approval or presale contracts confirming repayment capacity within proposed bridge term.

### Can developers use bridging finance for residential and commercial projects?

Yes, bridging finance accommodates both residential and commercial development projects though assessment criteria and pricing vary between categories. Residential development bridges including house-and-land, townhouse, or apartment projects attract most favourable terms with interest rates of 9-13% and maximum LVRs of 70-75%, reflecting established residential property markets providing strong security values and exit liquidity. Commercial development projects including office, retail, or industrial properties face more conservative lending with 65-70% maximum LVRs and rates of 11-15% reflecting narrower buyer markets, longer sales periods, and greater value volatility during market downturns. Mixed-use developments combining residential and commercial components receive blended assessment incorporating elements from both categories, typically achieving middle-ground terms between pure residential and commercial equivalents depending on residential versus commercial component proportions within total project scope.

### What happens if a development bridge cannot be repaid on time?

Development bridge default consequences depend on circumstances creating repayment difficulties and developer proactive engagement with lenders addressing problems. Initial communication when potential delays emerge enables collaborative solutions including term extensions, partial repayments reducing principal, or alternative security arrangements strengthening lender positions tolerating extended timelines. Extension fees of 0.5-1.5% plus rate increases of 1-3% apply to approved extensions, adding costs but preventing formal default proceedings. Unresolved defaults trigger formal default notices demanding full repayment, potentially followed by mortgagee possession and property sale proceedings if developers cannot remedy defaults within specified cure periods typically 30-90 days. Lenders prefer avoiding forced sales given value destruction from rushed marketing, often negotiating solutions including developer equity contributions, additional security provision, or controlled marketing programs maximising sale proceeds protecting lender interests whilst minimising developer losses.

### Should developers use bridging finance or wait for construction finance?

Decision depends on specific circumstances including site acquisition competitiveness, opportunity costs from delays, and bridging cost impacts on project feasibility. Competitive site acquisitions with multiple interested buyers or vendor preferences for unconditional offers favour bridging finance enabling immediate purchase commitment preventing lost opportunities to competitors offering faster settlement. Exclusive site negotiations or patient vendors accommodate extended settlement aligning with construction finance approval timelines of 12-16 weeks, potentially eliminating bridging finance needs and saving 8-15% of site cost in bridge fees and interest. However, opportunity costs from delayed project commencement potentially exceed bridging costs for favourable sites in appreciating markets where early construction completion captures buyer demand or value growth offsetting bridge expenses. Comprehensive analysis comparing bridge costs against opportunity costs, competitive positioning requirements, and project timeline implications determines optimal approach for specific scenarios rather than default positions favouring or avoiding bridging regardless of circumstances.
