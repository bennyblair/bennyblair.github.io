# Property Development Loans: Complete Funding Guide

Property development loans provide the capital needed to fund construction and development projects across Australia. From residential subdivisions and apartment buildings to commercial towers and mixed-use developments, these specialised facilities enable developers, builders, and investors to transform land into completed properties. This guide explains how development finance works, who qualifies, what lenders assess, and how to structure your next project for success.

## At a Glance: Key Takeaways

- **What are property development loans?** Specialised finance facilities that fund the construction phase of property projects, typically released in stages as building milestones are met.
- **Who uses them?** Property developers, builders, investors, and business owners undertaking construction projects with clear feasibility and exit strategies.
- **How do they work?** Funds are drawn down progressively against certified building stages, with interest capitalised during construction and repaid upon project completion and sale or refinance.
- **Key requirements:** Detailed feasibility study, experienced team, appropriate equity contribution, and clear exit strategy (sale or refinance).
- **Typical terms:** 12-36 month construction periods, 60-80% of total development costs funded, with interest rates reflecting project risk and developer experience.

## What Are Property Development Loans?

Property development loans are specialised construction finance facilities designed to fund the building phase of property projects. Unlike standard commercial mortgages that finance completed properties, development loans provide capital for the actual construction process—from site preparation and foundations through to final completion and certification.

These facilities are typically structured as progress payment loans, where funds are released in stages as certified building milestones are achieved. Interest is usually capitalised during construction (added to the loan balance) and repaid upon project completion, either through property sales or refinancing into a longer-term investment facility.

Development finance addresses the unique capital requirements of construction projects: high upfront costs, progressive capital deployment, and the gap between expenditure timing and revenue realisation. Lenders assess these facilities differently from standard property loans, focusing on project feasibility, builder credentials, presale levels (for residential), and exit strategy credibility.

## When Development Finance Makes Sense vs Alternatives

Development finance is specifically designed for construction projects where capital is needed to fund building works. Understanding when it's the right choice versus alternative financing options helps ensure you select the optimal structure for your project.

### Development Finance vs Standard Commercial Mortgage

**Choose development finance when:**
- You're constructing new buildings or substantially renovating existing structures
- You need progressive capital releases tied to building milestones
- The project has a defined construction period (typically 12-36 months)
- You plan to sell completed properties or refinance upon completion

**Choose a standard commercial mortgage when:**
- You're purchasing an already-completed property
- You need long-term investment finance (5+ years)
- The property generates immediate rental income
- You want fixed repayments from day one

### Development Finance vs Bridging Finance

**Choose development finance when:**
- You have a multi-stage construction project with certified milestones
- The project involves substantial building works over an extended period
- You need a facility specifically structured for construction progress payments
- You have detailed feasibility studies and professional team in place

**Choose bridging finance when:**
- You need short-term capital (typically 3-12 months) for timing gaps
- The facility is for acquisition, refinance, or holding purposes rather than construction
- You have a clear exit strategy that doesn't involve building completion
- The transaction is simpler with fewer moving parts than a full development

## How Property Development Loans Work: The Process

Understanding the development finance process helps you prepare effectively and manage lender expectations throughout your project.

### 1. Feasibility and Planning Phase
Before approaching lenders, complete detailed project feasibility including:
- **Cost estimates:** Quantity surveyor reports detailing construction costs, professional fees, holding costs, and contingencies
- **Development timeline:** Realistic construction schedule with key milestones
- **Market analysis:** Demand assessment, comparable sales, and pricing strategy
- **Exit strategy:** Clear plan for selling completed properties or refinancing into investment facilities

### 2. Lender Selection and Application
With feasibility complete, approach suitable development lenders:
- **Major banks:** Typically require strong developer track records, substantial presales (for residential), and lower-risk projects
- **Non-bank lenders:** Often more flexible on presales, developer experience, and project types
- **Private capital:** May fund higher-risk or more complex projects with appropriate structuring

Submit comprehensive application packages including feasibility studies, builder contracts, planning approvals, team credentials, and financial statements.

### 3. Due Diligence and Approval
Lenders conduct thorough due diligence:
- **Project assessment:** Review feasibility, market analysis, and exit strategy
- **Team evaluation:** Assess developer/builder experience and track record
- **Security review:** Evaluate land value, location, and development potential
- **Structure development:** Determine loan amount, interest rate, fees, and conditions

Approval typically involves conditional terms subject to valuation, legal documentation, and presale requirements (if applicable).

### 4. Documentation and Settlement
Once approved, legal documentation is prepared:
- **Loan agreement:** Details facility terms, drawdown conditions, and covenants
- **Security documents:** First mortgage over the land and development
- **Builder contracts:** Certified fixed-price contracts with appropriate insurances
- **Pre-settlement conditions:** Fulfill any remaining requirements (presales, equity injection, etc.)

Initial settlement releases funds for land acquisition (if not already owned) and early-stage works.

### 5. Construction and Drawdowns
During construction, funds are drawn progressively:
- **Milestone certification:** Quantity surveyor or project manager certifies completion of each building stage
- **Progress payments:** Lender releases funds against certified milestones
- **Interest capitalisation:** Interest accrues and is added to the loan balance during construction
- **Monitoring:** Lender may conduct site inspections and review progress reports

### 6. Completion and Exit
Upon project completion:
- **Final certification:** Building certifier issues occupancy certificate
- **Final drawdown:** Remaining funds released to complete project
- **Exit execution:** Properties are sold or refinanced as per the exit strategy
- **Loan repayment:** Facility repaid from sale proceeds or refinance funds

## What Lenders Assess in Development Finance Applications

Development lenders evaluate applications through multiple risk lenses to determine project viability and appropriate structuring.

### Project Feasibility and Viability
- **Cost accuracy:** Are construction estimates realistic with appropriate contingencies?
- **Market demand:** Is there genuine demand for the completed product at projected prices?
- **Timeline realism:** Is the construction schedule achievable given project complexity?
- **Profit margin:** Does the project generate sufficient profit to justify risk and provide buffer for contingencies?

### Developer/Builder Experience and Track Record
- **Previous projects:** Has the developer successfully completed similar projects?
- **Team credentials:** Do key personnel (project manager, builder, sales agent) have relevant experience?
- **Financial capacity:** Can the developer contribute required equity and absorb cost overruns?
- **Reputation:** Are there any history of disputes, delays, or quality issues?

### Security and Location
- **Land value:** What is the underlying land value relative to total project cost?
- **Location quality:** Is the site in a desirable area with strong market fundamentals?
- **Development potential:** Does the site have appropriate zoning, access, and services?
- **End value:** What is the projected completed value based on comparable sales?

### Exit Strategy and Presales
- **Sales plan:** Is there a realistic marketing and sales strategy for completed properties?
- **Presale levels:** For residential projects, what percentage is pre-sold (if required)?
- **Refinance option:** If holding as investment, is there a clear refinance path post-completion?
- **Market conditions:** Are exit assumptions reasonable given current and projected market conditions?

## Common Development Finance Structures

Different project types and risk profiles suit different financing structures. Understanding these options helps you select the optimal approach.

### Traditional Bank Development Finance
**Best for:** Established developers with strong track records, lower-risk projects, and ability to meet presale requirements.

**Typical terms:**
- 60-70% of total development costs (including land)
- 12-24 month construction periods
- 50-70% presales required for residential projects
- Competitive interest rates (margin over bank bill rate)
- Full recourse to developer

**Advantages:** Lower interest rates, established processes, relationship benefits for future projects.

**Considerations:** Stringent requirements, slower approval processes, less flexibility during construction.

### Non-Bank Development Finance
**Best for:** Developers with less extensive track records, more complex projects, or needing flexibility on presales.

**Typical terms:**
- 65-75% of total development costs
- 12-36 month construction periods
- Reduced or no presale requirements (assessed on end-value)
- Higher interest rates reflecting increased risk
- Often limited or non-recourse structures

**Advantages:** More flexible assessment, faster approvals, tailored structures for unique projects.

**Considerations:** Higher costs, potentially more complex documentation, shorter track records for some lenders.

### Joint Venture Equity Partnerships
**Best for:** Landowners with development sites but limited development capital or experience.

**Structure:** Land contributed as equity, development partner provides capital and expertise, profits shared according to agreed ratios.

**Advantages:** Enables development without selling land, leverages partner expertise, aligns interests through profit sharing.

**Considerations:** Complex legal structures, requires strong partnership alignment, profit sharing reduces developer returns.

### Mezzanine Development Finance
**Best for:** Developers needing additional capital beyond senior debt limits but wanting to avoid full equity dilution.

**Structure:** Subordinated debt layer between senior debt and equity, typically funding 5-15% of project costs.

**Advantages:** Increases total funding without full equity dilution, often unsecured or second mortgage position.

**Considerations:** Higher cost than senior debt, complex intercreditor arrangements, typically shorter terms.

## Key Documentation Requirements

Preparing comprehensive documentation streamlines the application process and demonstrates professional project management.

### Essential Documents for All Applications
- **Detailed feasibility study** including cost estimates, timeline, market analysis, and exit strategy
- **Developer financial statements** (last 2-3 years) and personal asset/liability statements
- **Land ownership documentation** (title search, planning certificates, contamination reports if applicable)
- **Planning approvals** (development consent, construction certificate, any conditions)
- **Builder fixed-price contract** with appropriate insurances (public liability, contract works, etc.)
- **Professional team CVs** highlighting relevant development experience

### Residential-Specific Requirements
- **Presale contracts** (if required by lender) with appropriate deposits
- **Marketing plan** detailing sales strategy, target market, and comparable sales
- **Display suite plans** and marketing materials (for larger projects)
- **Strata management arrangements** (for multi-unit developments)

### Commercial/Industrial-Specific Requirements
- **Lease agreements** or heads of agreement for pre-committed tenants
- **Market rental analysis** with comparable evidence
- **Due diligence reports** (environmental, geotechnical, traffic if applicable)
- **End-value assessment** by registered valuer

## Common Challenges and How to Address Them

Even well-planned developments face challenges. Proactive planning helps mitigate risks and maintain lender confidence.

### Cost Overruns
**Challenge:** Construction costs exceeding initial estimates, straining cash flow and profit margins.

**Mitigation strategies:**
- Include realistic contingencies (typically 5-10% for construction, 2-3% for professional fees)
- Use fixed-price builder contracts with clear scope definitions
- Regular cost monitoring and reporting throughout construction
- Maintain contingency funding access (equity reserve or contingency facility)

### Construction Delays
**Challenge:** Project timelines extending beyond schedule, increasing holding costs and potentially missing market windows.

**Mitigation strategies:**
- Build realistic timelines with buffer for common delays (weather, approvals, material availability)
- Include liquidated damages clauses in builder contracts
- Regular progress monitoring with early intervention on emerging issues
- Consider interest rate hedging if delays significantly extend finance term

### Presale Shortfalls (Residential)
**Challenge:** Failing to achieve required presale levels within specified timeframes.

**Mitigation strategies:**
- Develop realistic marketing plans with multiple sales channels
- Price competitively based on current market conditions
- Consider presale alternatives (bank guarantees, cash deposits, etc.) if lender allows
- Have backup plans (owner-occupier purchase, rental strategy) if presales don't materialise

### Market Downturns During Construction
**Challenge:** Property market softening between project commencement and completion, affecting exit values.

**Mitigation strategies:**
- Stress test feasibility against various market scenarios (base, optimistic, pessimistic)
- Maintain conservative valuation assumptions with buffer for market movements
- Consider pre-sale or forward sale arrangements to lock in prices
- Have flexible exit options (sell, hold, or stage releases based on market conditions)

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What equity contribution is typically required for development projects?
Most lenders require developers to contribute 20-35% of total development costs as equity. The exact percentage depends on project type, developer experience, presale levels, and market conditions. Equity can come from cash reserves, existing property equity, or joint venture partner contributions.

### How long does development finance approval take?
Simple projects with complete documentation can receive indicative approval within 2-3 weeks. Complex projects may require 4-8 weeks for full due diligence and approval. The timeline depends on project complexity, documentation completeness, and lender processes.

### Can first-time developers access development finance?
Yes, but with additional requirements. First-time developers typically need stronger professional teams (experienced builder, project manager), more conservative feasibility assumptions, higher equity contributions, and potentially personal guarantees. Some specialist lenders focus specifically on funding emerging developers.

### What happens if construction costs exceed the loan amount?
If costs exceed the approved loan amount, the developer must fund the shortfall from equity reserves. This is why realistic cost estimates with appropriate contingencies are critical. Some facilities include contingency allowances, but these are typically limited and may require lender approval to access.

### Can development finance cover land acquisition?
Yes, most development finance structures can include land acquisition costs, either as part of an integrated facility or as a separate land loan that converts to construction finance upon approval receipt and construction commencement. The total facility typically covers 60-80% of combined land and construction costs.

### What security do lenders take for development finance?
Lenders typically take a first registered mortgage over the land and development. They may also take security over presale deposits, builder performance bonds, and sometimes cross-collateralisation over other developer assets. Personal guarantees from directors are common, especially for smaller developers or higher-risk projects.

### How are interest payments handled during construction?
Interest is typically capitalised during construction—added to the loan balance rather than paid monthly. This recognises that the project isn't generating income during construction. Upon completion and sale or refinance, the accumulated interest is repaid along with the principal.

### What exit strategies do lenders prefer?
Lenders assess exit strategies based on credibility and market conditions. For residential projects, presales to genuine buyers provide the clearest exit. For commercial projects, pre-commitments from quality tenants or refinance based on completed value are common. The key is demonstrating a realistic path to full repayment within the facility term.
