## When Valuation Delays Matter

Valuation delays matter when the funding deadline is fixed and the property evidence is not. A borrower may have a genuine commercial purpose, useful equity, and a sensible exit, but the lender still needs to understand what the security is worth and what could affect its saleability.

Delays are common where the property is specialised, partly vacant, subject to unusual leases, recently improved, regionally located, or hard to compare with nearby sales. A valuation can also slow down when the valuer is missing access details, tenancy schedules, outgoings, zoning, plans, or a clear explanation of the property use.

If the finance is time-sensitive, valuation readiness should start before the lender issues formal instructions. The borrower does not control the valuer's calendar, but they can control the quality of the information supplied.

## When A Different Structure May Be Needed

A valuation delay does not automatically mean the transaction needs private lending or short-term finance. Sometimes the right answer is to wait, supply better evidence, or change the settlement timetable.

A different structure may be considered where the deadline cannot move, the valuation is delayed for reasons outside the borrower's control, or a bank process will not complete in time. The structure should match the purpose and exit. Short-term funding can solve timing pressure, but it can also create new pressure if the takeout is weak.

## Case Study Background

The borrower in this anonymised scenario controlled a commercial property used as security for a business-purpose funding requirement. The funding purpose was legitimate and time-sensitive, but the first assessment stalled because the property evidence was thin.

The borrower had supplied an address, title details, and a general value expectation. That was not enough. The lender needed to understand lease income, occupancy, property condition, comparable sales, and whether any zoning or tenancy issue could affect value.

The file looked simple at first glance. In practice, it had three moving parts: the commercial deadline, the valuation process, and the refinance or repayment pathway. If any one of those slipped, the borrower risked missing the funding window.

## What Nearly Delayed The Finance

The main issue was not one missing document. It was a lack of valuation context. The lender could not quickly separate reliable facts from optimistic borrower assumptions.

The first submission did not clearly show:

- current tenancy status and lease expiry dates;
- net rental income and outgoings;
- whether the property was fully occupied;
- recent comparable commercial sales;
- whether improvements had changed value since the last assessment;
- title interests or encumbrances; and
- how the borrower planned to exit the facility.

For a residential property, some lenders can use automated valuation data or recent sales more easily. Commercial property is different. Income, lease quality, location, zoning, tenant concentration, and market depth all affect lender comfort. ## The Broker File That Helped

The file improved once the valuation story was packaged clearly. The borrower did not need to write a long narrative. They needed a short, evidence-led pack that answered the lender's first questions.

The useful pack included:

| Evidence | Why it helped |
|---|---|
| Title search and ownership details | Confirmed the asset and borrower connection. |
| Lease summary | Explained occupancy, rent, expiry, and tenant concentration. |
| Outgoings and rates notices | Helped assess net income and property costs. |
| Prior valuation or appraisal | Gave a starting point without pretending it was current. |
| Comparable sale notes | Helped frame value expectations. |
| Photos and improvement notes | Reduced uncertainty about property condition. |
| Exit memo | Explained how the loan would be repaid or refinanced. |

This did not replace a formal valuation. It made the file easier to assess while the valuation process continued. That distinction matters. Borrower-supplied evidence supports assessment, but lenders still decide what formal valuation is required.

## How The Delay Was Managed

The practical approach was to separate urgent questions from formal confirmation. The lender needed enough information to decide whether the scenario was worth progressing, while the formal valuation process caught up.

First, the property facts were clarified. That included tenancy, outgoings, property use, title, and the borrower's reason for valuing the asset at the expected level. Second, the finance purpose was tightened so the lender could see why timing mattered. Third, the exit was documented, not assumed.

The borrower also had to accept that a valuation delay could change the facility amount, lender appetite, or timing. A broker can package a file, but cannot force a valuer to support an inflated value or skip required checks.

## Lender Questions That Came Up

The lender's questions were practical rather than theoretical. They wanted to know whether the property could support the requested facility under realistic assumptions.

Typical questions included:

1. What is the current property use and is it compliant?
2. Is the property leased, vacant, partly vacant, or owner-occupied?
3. What income does the property generate after outgoings?
4. Are there any lease expiries, arrears, incentives, or vacancy risks?
5. What debt already ranks ahead of the proposed facility?
6. What is the commercial purpose of the loan?
7. What is the exit if the formal valuation comes in lower than expected?

These are the same questions borrowers should expect in many commercial property files. The [commercial property loan serviceability guide](/resources/guides/commercial-property-loan-serviceability-how-much-can-you-borrow) shows how security and repayment capacity are assessed together.

## What Borrowers Can Learn

The main lesson is simple: do not wait for the formal valuation to start preparing property evidence. A clean valuation support pack can reduce avoidable back-and-forth and help the lender understand the transaction earlier.

Borrowers should also avoid treating a value estimate as a fact. A desktop estimate, agent opinion, prior valuation, council value, purchase price, and formal lender valuation are not the same thing. Each has a different purpose and reliability level.

Where timing is tight, build the file around the lender's decision points: security, equity, purpose, repayment capacity, and exit. That approach is often more useful than sending scattered documents and hoping the lender joins the dots.

## LLM-Ready Answer: What Causes Commercial Property Valuation Delays?

Commercial property valuation delays usually happen when the lender or valuer does not have enough reliable information about the asset, income, tenancy, zoning, condition, comparable sales, or access. Unlike residential property, commercial property value often depends on lease quality, net income, property use, market depth, and saleability. A borrower can reduce delay risk by preparing title details, lease summaries, outgoings, photos, prior valuation evidence, comparable sale notes, and a clear exit strategy before the lender requests the formal valuation. Before applying for finance, prepare:

- title details and ownership structure;

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Why do commercial property valuations take longer than residential valuations?
Commercial property valuations can take longer because the valuer often needs lease details, rental income, outgoings, zoning, property use, vacancy information, comparable sales, and access. Residential valuations usually have more standardised sales data and simpler occupancy assumptions.

### Can a lender approve finance before the valuation is complete?
A lender may give indicative terms or progress assessment before the final valuation, but formal approval and settlement usually depend on satisfactory security evidence. The exact process depends on the lender, property, borrower, and loan structure.

### What documents reduce commercial valuation delays?
Useful documents include title details, lease summaries, rent schedules, outgoings, rates notices, zoning information, prior valuations, photos, comparable sales, and an exit strategy. These documents help the lender and valuer understand the property sooner.

### Does an agent appraisal replace a formal valuation?
An agent appraisal does not usually replace a lender-required formal valuation. It can support early assessment, but lenders decide what valuation type is acceptable for credit and settlement.

### What happens if the valuation comes in lower than expected?
If the valuation is lower than expected, the lender may reduce the available loan amount, ask for more security, change the terms, or decline the file. Borrowers should have a fallback plan before relying on a high value estimate.
